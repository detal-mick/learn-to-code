Adverbs of Frequency
These adverbs tell us how often something happens. They form a scale from 0% (never) to 100% (always). Tap any adverb below to see it in context.
0% — never 50% — sometimes 100% — always
Example sentences
Comparative forms
"More often" and "most often" are used to compare frequency across people, places, or situations — not just to say how often something happens.
Base form
often
How frequently someone does something — no comparison
"She often recycles her bottles."
Comparative
more often
Comparing two people, places, or habits
"I recycle more often than my neighbour."
Superlative
most often
The highest frequency in a group of three or more
"Of us all, Sari recycles most often."

Multi-word Verbs
These verbs combine a base verb with one or two particles to create a new meaning. Tap any card to see the definition, an example sentence, and a usage tip.
Separable vs. inseparable
Some multi-word verbs can be separated by an object. For example:

throw away plastic  =  throw plastic away   ✓  (separable)

But with a pronoun, you must separate:  throw it away   ✓    throw away it   ✗

Inseparable verbs (like run out of, look after) must always stay together:  look after the planet   ✓
